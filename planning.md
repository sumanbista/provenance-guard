# Provenance Guard — Planning & Design Spec

Provenance Guard is a backend service a creative-sharing platform plugs in to answer one
question about a piece of submitted text: **was this written by a human, or generated by
AI?** It returns an attribution verdict, a calibrated confidence score, and a plain-language
transparency label — and it gives creators a way to appeal. It is *not* a content police:
its goal is honest attribution and trust, which means acknowledging uncertainty rather than
forcing a binary answer.

**Guiding principle — the false-positive asymmetry.** On a writing platform, wrongly branding
a *human's* work as AI-generated is the damaging error. It is more costly than failing to
catch a piece of AI text. This asymmetry is a design constraint that shows up concretely in
three places below: (1) the confidence thresholds are asymmetric — it takes *stronger*
evidence to say "AI" than to say "human"; (2) when the two signals disagree, we default to
*Uncertain*, never to an AI accusation; and (3) the AI label is worded to invite appeal.


**Submission flow (narrative).** A `POST /submit` request passes through the rate limiter and
a length validator, then into the detection pipeline where two independent signals — an
LLM-as-judge (semantic) and stylometric heuristics (structural) — each emit an AI-likelihood
in `[0,1]`. The confidence scorer blends them into a single verdict + confidence, the label
generator picks one of three transparency labels, and the whole decision is written to the
SQLite `audit_log` before the structured JSON response is returned.

**Appeal flow (narrative).** A `POST /appeal` request looks up the original submission by
`content_id`, records the creator's reasoning in the `appeals` table, flips the submission's
status to `under review`, and appends an appeal event to the `audit_log` — so the appeal
always lives next to the decision it contests. No automated re-classification happens; a
human reviewer takes it from there.

## 1. Detection Signals

The pipeline uses **two methodologically independent signals**. They are "distinct" in the
required sense because they measure different properties and *fail in different ways*: one is
semantic and model-based, the other structural and rule-based. Agreement between them is real
evidence; disagreement is our cue to report *Uncertain*.

### Signal 1 — LLM-as-judge (semantic), via Groq `llama-3.3-70b-versatile`

- **Measures:** whether the text *reads* as human vs. machine — coherence, phrasing, voice,
  hedging, presence/absence of idiosyncrasy.
- **Why it differs between human & AI:** AI prose trends toward smooth, generic,
  evenly-balanced coherence; human writing carries quirks, uneven emphasis, and opinion.
- **Output shape:** we prompt the model for a fixed, parseable format and extract a single
  `llm_score ∈ [0,1]` (probability the text is AI-generated) plus a one-line reason:

  ```
  AI_LIKELIHOOD: 0-100      # parsed and divided by 100 -> llm_score
  REASON: <one sentence>
  ```
- **Blind spot:** non-deterministic (same input → different verdicts); fooled by AI text that
  has been edited/"humanized" and by genuinely human text that is formal or generic. It is
  itself an LLM with no ground truth and can be confidently wrong.
- **Fail-closed default:** if the Groq call errors or the output can't be parsed, this signal
  returns `llm_score = 0.5` (maximally uncertain) rather than guessing — it must never push a
  submission toward an AI accusation on a parse failure.

### Signal 2 — Stylometric heuristics (structural), pure Python

Three metrics, each normalized to an AI-likelihood subscore in `[0,1]`, then averaged into
`sty_score`. Direction of each mapping: **more uniform / less varied → more AI-like.**
(Normalization constants below are initial values; they are tuned in Milestone 4.)

| Metric | What it measures | Human vs AI | Mapping to subscore |
|--------|------------------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Sentence-length burstiness** — coefficient of variation `CV = std/mean` of sentence lengths | rhythm / variability | humans are bursty (high CV); AI is uniform (low CV) | `sub = clamp((0.6 − CV) / (0.6 − 0.2), 0, 1)` |
| **Punctuation variety** — count of distinct marks used from `{, ; : — ( ) " ' ! ? …}` | expressive range | humans use more varied punctuation; AI is plainer | `sub = clamp((3 − distinct) / 3, 0, 1)` |
| **Lexical diversity (TTR)** — unique words / total words (on a fixed window) | vocabulary richness | extreme uniformity reads AI-ish | `sub = clamp((0.45 − TTR) / (0.45 − 0.20), 0, 1)` |

- **Output shape:** `sty_score ∈ [0,1]` plus the raw metric values (kept for the audit log).
- **Blind spot:** needs length to be stable; **genre-blind** — poetry, lists, and technical
  writing violate its assumptions, so a real human poem can look "too uniform." It ignores
  meaning entirely. Thresholds are hand-tuned heuristics, not learned. TTR is the noisiest of
  the three (its direction is length-sensitive), so it is weighted least in practice via the
  tuning in M4.

### Combining the signals

```
p_ai      = 0.6 * llm_score + 0.4 * sty_score        # weighted blend (LLM more reliable)
agreement = 1 − abs(llm_score − sty_score)           # ∈ [0,1], 1 = perfect agreement
```

The LLM gets the larger weight because it is the more capable classifier, but stylometry
keeps a meaningful 40% so a confident-but-wrong LLM can be pulled back toward *Uncertain* by a
disagreeing structural signal — which is exactly the false-positive protection we want.

## 2. Uncertainty Representation & Confidence Scoring

**What confidence *means* (decided before the math):** `confidence` is the system's confidence
in the verdict it reports, on a `[0,1]` scale.
- **`0.5` = "we genuinely cannot tell."** A coin flip. This is the honest center of the scale.
- **→ 1.0** = near-certain in the stated direction.
- So a confidence of **0.6** means *"leaning, but with real doubt"* — not enough to make an
  accusation. As shown below, 0.6 is deliberately on the boundary: it is *just* enough to
  affirm "human," but **not** enough to declare "AI." That difference is the asymmetry made
  numeric.

**Deriving the confidence score:**

```
base_conf  = abs(p_ai − 0.5) * 2                     # 0 at the midpoint, 1 at the extremes
confidence = base_conf * (0.5 + 0.5 * agreement)     # signal disagreement halves confidence
direction  = "AI" if p_ai >= 0.5 else "human"
```

Multiplying by `(0.5 + 0.5·agreement)` means two signals that flatly contradict each other can
never yield high confidence, even if the blend happens to land at an extreme.

**Thresholds — asymmetric on purpose.** The verdict/label is chosen by confidence *bands*
that differ by direction:

| Condition (evaluated in order) | Verdict → Label variant |
|--------------------------------|-------------------------|
| `word_count < 40` (too short to analyze reliably) | **Uncertain** (confidence capped at 0.30) |
| `direction == "AI"` **and** `confidence ≥ 0.75` **and** `agreement ≥ 0.5` | **Likely AI-generated** |
| `direction == "human"` **and** `confidence ≥ 0.60` | **Likely human-written** |
| anything else | **Uncertain** |

The AI branch requires a *higher* confidence (0.75 vs 0.60) **and** signal agreement; the
human branch does not. So a high `p_ai` produced by only one signal (e.g. stylometry flagging
a repetitive poem while the LLM says human) falls through to *Uncertain* instead of accusing a
human. A `0.51` confidence always lands in *Uncertain*; a `0.95` clears either bar — the two
produce meaningfully different labels, as required.

**How we'll test the scores are meaningful (Milestone 4):** run a small labeled set — clearly
human samples (personal essays, messy forum posts), clearly AI samples (raw ChatGPT output),
and known-hard cases (formal academic prose, repetitive poetry) — and confirm (a) clear-human
and clear-AI land at opposite ends with high confidence, (b) the hard cases land in
*Uncertain* rather than being confidently misclassified, and (c) scores *move* between
categories rather than clustering at one value.

## 3. Transparency Label Design

Three variants. Each shows a plain-language verdict, the confidence as a percentage, and an
honest caveat. The **AI** variant carries the most hedging and an explicit appeal invitation
(the asymmetry); the **human** variant is affirming; the **uncertain** variant makes clear no
determination was made. `{conf}` is `round(confidence * 100)`.

**High-confidence AI:**
> 🤖 **Likely AI-generated.** Multiple independent signals suggest this text was probably
> produced by an AI system (our certainty: {conf}%). Automated detection is not perfect and
> can misclassify original human writing. If you wrote this yourself, you can appeal this
> result and a person will review it.

**High-confidence human:**
> ✍️ **Likely written by a human.** Our analysis found the writing patterns and style
> consistent with human authorship (our certainty: {conf}%). This is an automated assessment,
> not a guarantee. If you believe it's wrong, you can appeal.

**Uncertain:**
> ❓ **Attribution uncertain.** Our signals disagree or aren't strong enough to reliably
> determine whether this text was written by a human or generated by AI (our certainty:
> {conf}%). We are **not** making a determination — please treat this as inconclusive rather
> than as evidence either way.

*(The verbatim text of these three variants is also reproduced in the README, which is the
canonical record for grading.)*

## 4. Appeals Workflow

- **Who can appeal:** the creator of the content — identified by the `content_id` returned at
  submission (and an optional `creator_id`). Anyone holding the `content_id` for a piece may
  contest its classification.
- **What they provide:** `content_id`, a free-text `reason` explaining why they believe the
  classification is wrong, and optionally a `creator_id`.
- **What the system does on receipt of an appeal:**
  1. Look up the original submission by `content_id`; return `404` if it doesn't exist.
  2. Insert a row into `appeals` (`appeal_id`, `content_id`, `reason`, `creator_id`, timestamp).
  3. Update the submission's `status` from `classified` → **`under review`**.
  4. Append an `audit_log` event `{event: "appeal"}` containing the reason and a reference to
     the original decision, so the appeal is stored *next to* what it contests.
  5. Return `{content_id, appeal_id, status: "under review"}`.
- **What a human reviewer sees in the appeal queue:** for each appeal — the original text, the
  original verdict, its confidence, **both signal scores** (so they can see *why* the system
  decided what it did), the creator's stated reason, the timestamps, and the current status.
  This is everything needed to re-adjudicate by hand. (Surfaced via the `audit_log` / a
  `GET /appeals`-style view.)

## 5. Anticipated Edge Cases

Specific scenarios the system will handle poorly, and how the design responds:

1. **Repetitive, simple-vocabulary poetry** (villanelle, nursery rhyme, chant). Short
   near-equal lines → low sentence-length CV *and* low TTR → stylometry scores it AI-like.
   *Response:* the LLM will likely still read it as human, the signals disagree, and the
   asymmetric threshold (AI needs confidence ≥ 0.75 **and** agreement ≥ 0.5) routes it to
   *Uncertain* instead of an AI accusation. This is the canonical false-positive case and the
   reason the asymmetry exists.

2. **Very short submissions** (a haiku, a two-sentence excerpt, a tweet-length post). Fewer
   than ~40 words makes both stylometric metrics unstable and the LLM unreliable.
   *Response:* the length gate forces *Uncertain* with confidence capped at 0.30 — the system
   refuses to pretend it can judge from too little text.

3. **Highly formal / technical human writing** (legal, academic, corporate boilerplate).
   Uniform sentence structure and a deliberately impersonal voice look AI-like to *both*
   signals, so this is our hardest genuine false-positive risk. *Response:* the raised AI
   threshold reduces (does not eliminate) the risk; this is a documented known weakness, and
   the appeal path is the safety net.

4. **Lightly human-edited AI text ("humanized").** Both signals may read human → a false
   *negative*. We accept this: perfect AI detection is unsolved, and we would rather miss some
   AI than falsely accuse a human. Documented honestly rather than hidden.

## API Surface (contract)

The server runs on **port 5001** (macOS AirPlay Receiver occupies the Flask default of 5000).

| Method | Endpoint | Accepts | Returns |
|--------|----------|---------|---------|
| `POST` | `/submit` | `{ "text": str, "creator_id"?: str }` (also accepts `"content"` as an alias) | `{ content_id, attribution, confidence, label, signals: {llm, stylometry}, status }` |
| `POST` | `/appeal` | `{ "content_id": str, "reason": str, "creator_id"?: str }` | `{ content_id, appeal_id, status: "under review" }` |
| `GET`  | `/log` | — | `{ "entries": [...] }` — recent `audit_log` rows, newest first (≥3 for grading) |
| `GET`  | `/status/<content_id>` | — | current status + stored decision *(optional convenience)* |
| `GET`  | `/health` | — | liveness check |

**`audit_log` columns (as built in Milestone 3):** `id`, `content_id`, `creator_id`,
`timestamp` (ISO-8601 UTC, `Z`-suffixed), `event` (`"submission"` | `"appeal"`),
`attribution`, `confidence`, `llm_score`, `status`. The log is extended with the stylometry
score in M4.

## AI Tool Plan

For each implementation milestone: which spec sections I hand to the AI tool, what I ask it
to generate, and how I verify the result before moving on.

### M3 — Submission endpoint + first signal
- **Spec sections provided:** *Detection Signals → Signal 1*, the *Architecture* diagram, and
  the *API Surface* row for `/submit`.
- **Ask it to generate:** a Flask app skeleton (`app.py`), `config.py` (Groq key, model,
  constants), and the `classify_llm(text) -> {"llm_score", "reason"}` function, wired so
  `/submit` returns the LLM signal only for now.
- **Verify:** call `classify_llm()` directly on a few clearly-human and clearly-AI samples in
  isolation *before* trusting the endpoint; confirm scores move in the right direction and the
  parse/fail-closed path returns 0.5 on a malformed response.

### M4 — Second signal + confidence scoring
- **Spec sections provided:** *Detection Signals → Signal 2* + *Combining the signals*,
  *Uncertainty Representation & Confidence Scoring*, and the diagram.
- **Ask it to generate:** the pure-Python `stylometry(text) -> {"sty_score", metrics}`
  function and the `score(llm_score, sty_score, word_count) -> {p_ai, agreement, confidence,
  direction, attribution}` combiner, then wire both into `/submit`.
- **Check:** run the labeled test set from §2 — do scores *vary meaningfully* between clearly
  AI and clearly human text? Do the repetitive-poem and short-text cases land in *Uncertain*?
  Tune the stylometry normalization constants and weights until they do.

### M5 — Production layer (labels, appeals, rate limiting, audit log)
- **Spec sections provided:** *Transparency Label Design*, *Appeals Workflow*, the *API
  Surface*, and the diagram.
- **Ask it to generate:** the `label_for(direction, confidence, agreement, word_count)`
  generator (the three variants), the SQLite schema + audit logging, Flask-Limiter config on
  `/submit`, and the `POST /appeal`, `GET /log` endpoints.
- **Verify:** craft inputs that reach **all three** label variants; confirm an appeal flips
  status to `under review` and writes both an `appeals` row and an `audit_log` event; confirm
  the rate limit returns `429` when exceeded; confirm `GET /log` shows ≥3 structured entries.
